The Main Event
The most famous comedy club in the world — and tonight, you are going for the first time. Since 1959, Second City has launched more comedic careers than any institution on earth. About Pilsen

Originally Czech and Bohemian in the late 1800s — Pilsen is named after a city in what is now the Czech Republic. By the 1950s and '60s, Mexican families arrived and transformed it into the cultural heart of Chicago's Mexican community. The murals are not decoration. They are memory, protest, and identity painted on walls by people who built a life in a city that did not always make it easy.

I arrived in this city over 25 years ago with 500 euros and built my life the same way — one neighbourhood at a time. Pilsen is that story told in colour, for everyone to see.

18th Street Mural Walk
Free · 40 min
Step off the train and you are already inside the gallery — the 18th Street station itself has murals on its walls. Walk east along 18th Street. Nearly every block is a different work: portraits of César Chávez and Mexican revolutionary figures, Aztec imagery woven into scenes of modern Chicago life, murals mourning loved ones lost to violence or immigration enforcement, work by neighbourhood youth alongside established artists. These were not commissioned by a museum. They were painted by residents, for residents. That is the whole point.
🌽 Aztec Roots Much of the imagery draws from Aztec culture — one of the great ancient civilisations, centred in what is now Mexico City. Quetzalcoatl, the sun calendar, warriors in ceremonial dress: a community reclaiming ancestry that colonisation tried to erase. It is still here, on the walls of a Chicago neighbourhood.
👤 César Chávez — A Complex Legacy You will likely see his portrait. Co-founder of the United Farm Workers in the 1960s, a genuine force for exploited farmworkers. In March 2026, a New York Times investigation published credible allegations of serious sexual abuse — including accounts from women abused as teenagers, and a statement from his co-founder Dolores Huerta. Cities have removed statues, renamed schools and streets. The movement was real. The harm was also real. The community is still working through what it means.
🧡 Immigration Now Mexican immigrants have done essential work in this country for over a century — in fields, restaurants, construction — often invisible, often exploited. Under expanded ICE enforcement since 2025, over 70% of those detained have no criminal record. Raids have hit workplaces, churches, homes. Chicago has been targeted. The people you walk past today live with that. The murals are not just art — they are defiance. We are here.
📸 Walk east from the station on 18th St — best murals concentrate in the first 8 blocks.
🆓 Free, open air. Early morning has the best light and quietest streets.

Hector Duarte Studio Mural
Free · 15 min
One building-sized work you will not forget. Duarte is one of Pilsen's most celebrated muralists, and his building on Cullerton depicts the destruction of the monarch butterfly population by GMO agriculture — a metaphor for the destruction of Mexican land, culture, and identity. It is visually stunning and intellectually layered. Stand in front of it and just look. Let it land.
📍 Located on Cullerton St near Racine Ave, Pilsen.

National Museum of Mexican Art
Free · 90–120 min
Free admission. Rated 4.8 stars with nearly 4,000 reviews — this is genuinely one of the finest art museums in Chicago, period. The permanent collection spans over 3,000 years of Mexican and Mexican-American art, from pre-Columbian artifacts to contemporary paintings. The Día de los Muertos installations have moved multiple visitors to tears. The staff is warm and deeply knowledgeable. Budget at least 90 minutes.
💛 From S — La Catrina This museum is where I learned the real story of La Catrina — the skull-faced woman in the wide feathered hat. Created around 1910 by engraver José Guadalupe Posada as satire: a skeleton dressed in European high-society fashion, mocking Mexican women who rejected their indigenous roots to imitate European aristocracy. Death comes for everyone equally — no finery saves you. The image languished until 1947, when Diego Rivera placed her at the centre of his greatest mural, connecting her to Aztec mythology and Frida Kahlo. She became a national symbol. Today she is on altars, faces, films, and Pixar movies. She started as a penny broadside. That is the full arc.
🆓 Always free. Open Tue–Sun 10 AM – 5 PM.
📍 1852 W 19th St, Pilsen.
nationalmuseumofmexicanart.org ↗

About Chinatown

Dating to the early 1900s, when Chinese residents were pushed out of downtown by rising rents and anti-Chinese hostility. They settled here and built a community that has lasted over a century — unlike many American Chinatowns that have faded, Chicago's has stayed alive and grown. These streets are the product of people who came with little and refused to let go. As someone who did the same thing over 25 years ago, I understand that more than I can easily say. Two zones: old Wentworth Avenue (traditional, family-owned) and the newer Chinatown Square plaza (more modern). Both worth your time.
Afternoon · Chinatown
2:20
PM
Chinatown Gateway Arch
Free · Best Photo Spot #1
The iconic red-and-gold arch on Wentworth Avenue, built in 1975 — the symbolic entrance to the neighbourhood and one of the most photographed spots on Chicago's South Side. Stand under it with the street unfurling behind you. This is your arrival shot.
🏮 Why Do All Chinatowns Have an Arch? It is called a paifang — a traditional Chinese gate, originally marking the entrance to an administrative district in ancient Chinese cities. In America, it became the visual declaration of a Chinese community's presence: we are here, this is our space. Some were donated by China or Taiwan as diplomatic gestures; Chicago's was sponsored by Taiwan in 1975. You have already seen San Francisco's. The story is the same here — a community claiming its place permanently.
📸 Face north from under the arch for the classic Wentworth Ave depth-of-field shot.
🚇 A 3 min walk south from the Cermak-Chinatown Red Line station.

Wentworth Avenue Stroll
Free · 30 min
Walk south along Wentworth. Peek into herbalist shops with jars of dried mushrooms and roots lining the walls. Spot roast ducks hanging in bakery windows. 💛 From S — On Those Ducks in the Window I had Peking duck the traditional way in Beijing. The real version: roasted whole, skin paper-thin and perfectly crisp, sliced tableside. You eat it with bao buns, oyster sauce, and — this surprised me — sugar. Yes, sugar. It sounds strange. It is absolutely right. When the meat is done, the carcass becomes bone broth soup. Every part used. Nothing wasted. One of those meals that makes you understand a culture through food.
🥐 Try a pineapple bun from any of the bakeries along the strip — sweet, soft, and perfect.

Two of Chicago's most charming neighbourhood high streets — one rooted in German and European immigrant history, the other in Scandinavian heritage and now one of the city's most vibrant and welcoming communities. About This Guide ＋

Chicago is not downtown. The real Chicago is its neighbourhoods — 77 official community areas, anywhere from 200 to 240 distinct neighbourhoods in total, boundaries constantly argued about. Chicagoans don't say they live in Chicago. They say they live in Wicker Park, or Pilsen, or Lincoln Square. The neighbourhood is the identity.

I have been here for over 25 years — Edgewater, Mayfair, Lakeview, Lincoln Square, now Evanston. What I learned: Chicago's diversity is not a talking point. It is the whole thing. I chose three neighbourhoods for you: one I know well as a Chicagoan, one that shows what art looks like when it comes from survival, and one where a century-old immigrant community still thrives and feeds you magnificently.

Three days. Three different Chicagos. All of them real.

A note from S

For Sister —

I came to Chicago over 25 years ago with 500 euros, a readiness to work hard, and the stubborn belief that it would eventually add up to something. It did. But it took time, and more of me than I knew I had to give.

In the many years since, I have moved through different neighborhoods across the city, and now I live in Evanston where you are sleeping this weekend. Every neighbourhood taught me something. Edgewater taught me loneliness and the size of this country. Lakeview taught me that a city can hold you. Lincoln Square taught me that immigrant communities build beautiful things not despite hardship but because of it. Evanston taught me what it finally feels like to be at home.

Chicago's real character is its people — the ones who painted Pilsen's murals, kept Chinatown alive for a century, turned Lincoln Square into something worth returning to. Immigrants, all of them, doing the same thing I did: showing up and refusing to quit.

I made this guide because I love you and want you to see what I see — not the postcard, the real thing. If N changes everything and the weekend looks nothing like this, that is fine. There are years ahead of us. Come back every year until you have done all of it.

I love this country — the openness, the belief that you can arrive with nothing and build a life. I am not blind to what is not good here. I have watched it wrestle with itself for many years. I think about where we came from — Europe, colonisation, what western culture built and what it took. Complicated. But this city, these people — proof that something real can grow in complicated soil.

Come back whenever you want. The city will still be here. So will I.

With love,
S
